Thought for 16s
In biology the umbrella term is tropism — directional growth guided by a stimulus (so it’s literally “growing a path” rather than walking one).

For the specific “roots / fungi tendrils seeking out routes” behaviors, the usual names are:

Chemotropism — growth steered by chemical gradients (nutrients, signals, pheromones). This is commonly used for fungal hyphae steering growth. 

Hydrotropism — roots bias growth toward higher moisture / water potential gradients (“roots searching for water”). 

Thigmotropism — growth steered by touch / contact / obstacles (common in tendrils; also observed as touch-guided responses in other plants and fungi). 

If you mean the overall network-level behavior (especially for fungi), papers often call it mycelial foraging / hyphal exploration (and even categorize styles like “guerrilla” vs “phalanx” exploration).

Thought for 19s

In fungi, the “weaving” is basically hyphae growing, branching, and then fusing into a load-bearing network.

The core “weave” processes (real terms)

Hyphal growth / apical extension: tips extend and branch to explore (the raw “thread laying”).

Hyphal anastomosis: hyphae fuse with other hyphae, creating channels and turning the colony into an interconnected network (this is the closest literal analog to “weaving threads together”).

Mycelium: the resulting mass of interwoven filamentous hyphae (the “cloth”).

Cording / mycelial cords (often rhizomorphs): bundled, parallel hyphae that form “cables” for long-distance exploration + transport—very “routes/arteries” in a graph.

Pseudoparenchyma (tissue made of densely interwoven hyphae): when the weave becomes more “solid”/tissue-like.

A fun etymology win for your naming

Hypha comes from Ancient Greek ὑφή (huphḗ) meaning “web” — fungi are literally “web-things” by name.
